# Post-Discharge Planner

## 1. When to Use

Trigger this skill when:
- Patient is being discharged or is about to be discharged from hospital
- Caregiver asks "what do we do after leaving the hospital?"
- Patient needs follow-up care instructions after surgery, procedure, or acute illness
- Doctor has given a discharge summary but the family needs it explained in plain language

---

## 2. Medication List

Collect or confirm the following for every medication on the discharge sheet:

| Field | What to Confirm |
|---|---|
| Drug name | Generic name + brand name if given |
| Dose | Exact amount per dose (e.g., 5 mg, not "one tablet") |
| Timing | Morning/afternoon/evening/night; before or after food |
| Duration | Number of days or "until review" |
| Special instructions | With water only, avoid sunlight, store in fridge |

Ask the discharging nurse or pharmacist to explain any medication the caregiver is unsure about.
Do not stop any medication without doctor approval, even if patient "feels better."

---

## 3. Follow-Up Timeline

Route follow-up based on condition severity:

- **7-day review**: Post-surgery (wound check), post-acute cardiac event, new diabetes/hypertension diagnosis, post-hospitalization infection
- **1-month review**: Stable chronic disease start, post-chemotherapy cycle, post-fracture (X-ray check)
- **3-month review**: Stable diabetes/hypertension management, post-TB treatment milestone, routine cancer surveillance

Book the appointment before leaving the hospital. Many government hospitals allow OPD pre-booking at the billing counter on discharge day.

---

## 4. Condition-Specific Instructions

**Post-Cardiac Surgery**
- No driving for 6 weeks; no lifting objects above 5 kg for 8 weeks
- INR monitoring: if on warfarin, INR test every 7–10 days until stable; ask for referral to anticoagulation clinic
- Sternal wound: keep dry; no direct shower spray on chest for 3 weeks
- Cardiac rehab referral: ask before leaving

**Post-Dialysis Start (CKD patients beginning regular dialysis)**
- Hemodialysis schedule: typically 3 sessions per week, 4 hours each
- Fluid restriction: usually 500 ml/day + previous day's urine output — confirm exact limit with nephrologist
- Dietary restrictions: low potassium (avoid banana, coconut water, tomato), low phosphorus (limit dairy, nuts)
- Fistula/catheter care: keep access site clean and dry; do not allow BP cuff on fistula arm

**Post-Chemotherapy Cycle**
- Infection watch: absolute neutrophil count may be lowest 7–14 days after chemo (nadir)
- Temperature ≥ 38°C → go to nearest ER immediately; do not wait to see if fever resolves
- Avoid crowded places for 10 days post-chemo
- Mouth sores: use prescribed mouthwash; soft diet only

---

## 5. Activity Restrictions

- Confirm specific restrictions given by the treating doctor; do not give generic advice
- Common examples: no stairs for 2 weeks post-abdominal surgery; no heavy work for 6 weeks post-caesarean; bed rest with limb elevation post-DVT treatment
- Return to work: ask doctor explicitly — do not assume patient can return when they "feel ready"

---

## 6. Diet Instructions

- Confirm dietary restrictions from dietician or doctor before leaving
- Surface any food-drug interactions (e.g., avoid grapefruit with certain cardiac medications; limit vitamin K foods with warfarin)
- Flag if patient needs dietician referral for long-term diet management (diabetes, CKD, post-bariatric surgery)

---

## 7. Red Flag Symptoms — Return to ER Immediately

Present this list clearly to caregiver. If any of the following occur, go to ER without delay:

- Fever above 38°C (especially in post-chemo, post-surgery, or immunocompromised patients)
- Chest pain or sudden shortness of breath
- Sudden severe headache or loss of consciousness
- Wound that is red, swollen, warm, or discharging pus
- Inability to urinate for more than 8 hours
- Vomiting that prevents keeping any medication down
- Leg swelling with pain (possible DVT)
- Confusion or sudden change in mental state

---

## 8. Who to Call

- Treating doctor's outpatient number or hospital helpline (confirm this number before discharge)
- Hospital nursing station where patient was admitted (for clinical queries in the first 48 hours)
- ASHA/ANM (for rural patients): community health worker can assist with follow-up coordination
- National Health Helpline: 104 (available in most states for health guidance)

---

## Guardrails

- Never modify or reinterpret the doctor's discharge instructions; only help the caregiver understand and organize them
- Do not recommend stopping or adjusting any medication
- If patient condition worsens at any point, direct to ER — do not attempt to troubleshoot acute symptoms through this app
- Discharge planning is a complement to clinical care, not a substitute for it

---

## Verify

- [ ] Medication list is complete with name, dose, timing, and duration for each drug
- [ ] At least one follow-up appointment has been booked or scheduled
- [ ] Caregiver can recite at least three red flag symptoms without prompting
- [ ] Condition-specific restrictions (driving, lifting, diet, fluid) have been communicated
- [ ] Emergency contact number has been confirmed and saved
