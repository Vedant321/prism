# Second Opinion Finder

## 1. When to Use

Use this skill when a patient has already received a diagnosis and a proposed treatment plan
from one doctor or hospital and wishes to have that opinion validated or challenged at a
different institution. Triggers include:

- The patient or family is uncertain about a serious diagnosis (cancer, major surgery,
  organ transplant, long-term medication for a chronic condition).
- The proposed treatment is high-risk, irreversible, or very costly (e.g., coronary bypass,
  amputation, chemotherapy regimen, spinal fusion).
- The patient feels the first opinion was rushed or inadequately explained.
- Test results from the first hospital are inconsistent with the patient's symptoms or
  earlier investigations.
- A GP or family member has proactively suggested seeking another view.

Do not use this skill when the patient is still in an acute emergency and delay would cause
harm — stabilise first, second opinion after.

## 2. Information Gathering

Collect the following before identifying a second opinion provider:

- **Existing diagnosis**: confirmed diagnosis as written on the discharge summary or
  consultation note — use the doctor's exact wording, not the patient's interpretation.
- **Proposed treatment plan**: surgery, chemotherapy, medication, watchful waiting, etc.
- **Current hospital and doctor**: name, city, and specialty of the first opinion doctor.
- **Hospital group / chain**: needed to flag intra-group conflicts (see Section 3).
- **Documents available**: list what the patient currently holds — discharge summary,
  biopsy / pathology report, imaging CDs, blood reports, prescriptions.
- **Patient location**: city of residence and ability to travel.
- **Budget**: self-pay ceiling per consultation, or insurance / government scheme coverage.
- **Timeline**: urgency of the treatment decision — days, weeks, or months available.

## 3. Institution Selection Rules

Apply the following rules in order when shortlisting a second opinion facility:

1. **Prefer teaching hospitals and tertiary care centres.** Government medical college
   hospitals (AIIMS, PGIMER, JIPMER, SGPGI, KEM, Safdarjung, etc.) and large accredited
   private tertiary hospitals (Tata Memorial, CMC Vellore, Manipal Hospitals, Medanta,
   Fortis, Apollo — at their flagship campuses) have tumour boards, multidisciplinary
   teams, and senior faculty who routinely provide second opinions.

2. **Flag intra-group second opinions and exclude them.** If the first opinion was at
   Apollo Chennai, do not suggest Apollo Delhi or any other Apollo-network hospital.
   If the first opinion was at a Fortis facility, exclude all Fortis hospitals. Intra-group
   opinions share protocols, empanelled pathology labs, and sometimes staff rotations —
   they do not constitute an independent view. State this reason explicitly to the patient.

3. **Never suggest the same hospital as the first opinion**, even if a different department
   or doctor is involved.

4. **Prefer NABH- or JCI-accredited institutions** where available. NABH (National
   Accreditation Board for Hospitals) accreditation is the Indian standard; JCI is the
   international benchmark. Both indicate audited clinical processes.

5. **Consider telemedicine second opinion** from premier institutions (AIIMS Delhi, Tata
   Memorial, CMC Vellore) when the patient cannot travel — applicable for review of
   pathology slides, imaging, and case records.

## 4. Specialist Matching

The second opinion doctor must match the primary specialty of the first opinion doctor:

- If the first opinion was from a surgical oncologist recommending tumour removal, the
  second opinion should be from another surgical oncologist — not a medical oncologist or
  radiation oncologist, unless the patient specifically wants to explore non-surgical
  alternatives (flag this clearly as a cross-specialty view, not a like-for-like opinion).
- If the first opinion was from a spine surgeon, the second opinion should be from another
  spine surgeon, not a general orthopaedic surgeon or neurologist.
- If the first opinion involved a multi-disciplinary tumour board decision, note this to
  the patient: a single second opinion doctor may not replicate that board setting; the
  patient may need to request another tumour board review at the second institution.
- Paediatric cases: match paediatric sub-specialty; do not route to an adult specialist
  for the second opinion even if the first doctor was adult-trained.

## 5. Documents to Bring

Advise the patient to carry all of the following to the second opinion appointment:

- Discharge summary from the current hospitalisation or the consultation note where
  the diagnosis was given.
- Biopsy or histopathology report (original, with laboratory letterhead and pathologist
  signature). For cancer cases, the second institution may request the paraffin-embedded
  tissue block for independent slide review.
- Imaging CDs or digital copies: CT scan, MRI, PET-CT, X-ray, ultrasound — all series,
  not just the radiology report. Many teaching hospitals re-read images independently.
- Blood test reports from the last 3 to 6 months: CBC, LFT, RFT, tumour markers, or
  other condition-specific panels.
- Full current prescription list with dosages.
- Referral letter from the treating doctor if available — not mandatory but helps frame
  the case.
- Originals plus two sets of photocopies of every document. Originals are returned; one
  photocopy is kept by the second opinion hospital, one stays with the patient.

## 6. How to Frame the Request

Coach the patient on how to present at the second opinion appointment:

- The recommended opening statement is: "I would like a second opinion on my diagnosis
  and treatment plan." This is a factual, professional request that doctors routinely
  accommodate.
- The patient should not say "my first doctor was wrong" or volunteer criticisms of the
  first doctor — this can put the second doctor in an awkward position and shift the
  consultation toward defending or attacking the first opinion rather than evaluating the
  clinical picture.
- Specific questions the patient should prepare and ask:
  - Is the diagnosis correct based on the records provided?
  - Is the proposed treatment the most appropriate option for my case?
  - Are there alternative treatment options, and what are the trade-offs?
  - What would you recommend if I were your patient?
  - Are there any additional investigations you would do before deciding on treatment?
- After the appointment: get the second opinion in writing (consultation note or letter)
  before leaving the hospital or request it by post / email within a week.

## 7. Guardrails

- Do not tell the patient which doctor's opinion is correct — present the second opinion
  as additional information for the patient and their family to discuss with both doctors.
- Do not suggest that seeking a second opinion implies the first doctor was negligent or
  incompetent; frame it as standard practice for serious diagnoses.
- Do not recommend a specific named doctor; recommend the specialist type and institution.
- If the patient has a time-sensitive condition (e.g., rapidly progressing cancer, impending
  organ failure), note the urgency and suggest the patient inform the second opinion
  provider of the timeline so the appointment is prioritised appropriately.
- Do not advise the patient to withhold information from either doctor — full transparency
  with both providers is essential for accurate opinions.
- Verify that recommended institutions accept walk-in second opinion requests or require
  a prior appointment, and advise the patient accordingly.

## 8. Verify

- [ ] Existing diagnosis and treatment plan are captured using the doctor's exact wording.
- [ ] First opinion hospital and hospital group identified to enable conflict-of-interest check.
- [ ] No institution from the same hospital group as the first opinion has been suggested.
- [ ] First opinion hospital itself is not included in suggestions.
- [ ] At least one NABH- or JCI-accredited institution is in the shortlist.
- [ ] Second opinion specialist matches the primary specialty of the first opinion doctor.
- [ ] Patient has been advised on which documents to carry, including imaging CDs.
- [ ] Patient has been coached on how to frame the request at the appointment.
- [ ] Written second opinion note has been flagged as a required output from the appointment.
- [ ] Timeline urgency has been communicated to the patient and accounts for document prep time.
