# Caregiver Guide

## 1. When to Use

Trigger this skill when:
- A family member or friend is accompanying a patient to hospital and asks "what should I bring?" or "what should I do?"
- Caregiver is unfamiliar with hospital admission process
- First-time caregiver managing paperwork, insurance, or ICU protocols
- Caregiver asks for help understanding what questions to ask the doctor

---

## 2. What to Bring

**Documents (carry physical copies + store photos on phone)**
- Aadhaar card (patient and one caregiver)
- Previous hospital records, discharge summaries, investigation reports
- Referral letter from treating doctor (if applicable)
- Insurance card / TPA card / Ayushman Bharat / CGHS card
- Blood group card or report
- List of current medications with doses

**Medications**
- All current medications in original packaging (do not decant into unlabeled containers)
- Bring enough supply for 5 days even if surgery is planned for 2

**Personal Items (pack for 3 nights minimum)**
- Comfortable loose clothing × 2 sets
- Towel, basic toiletries (hospital may not provide)
- Phone charger + power bank
- Snacks for yourself — hospital canteens may close at night
- Small amount of cash for incidentals (even if insured)

---

## 3. Questions to Ask the Doctor

At every significant interaction, caregiver should ask and note the answers:

1. **Diagnosis**: "What is the condition and what does it mean in simple terms?"
2. **Treatment plan**: "What will be done and in what order? How long will it take?"
3. **Alternatives**: "Are there other treatment options? What are the risks and benefits of each?"
4. **Prognosis**: "What is the expected outcome? What is the recovery timeline?"
5. **What to watch for at home**: "If we see [X], should we come back immediately?"
6. **Discharge criteria**: "What needs to happen before the patient can go home?"

Write answers down. Request a second opinion if the diagnosis is serious — this is your right as a patient/caregiver.

---

## 4. Managing Paperwork

**Admission**
- Fill admission form completely; errors cause billing and insurance delays
- Carry one extra set of document photocopies — many hospitals ask for copies at multiple counters

**Consent Forms**
- Read before signing; ask for explanation of any procedure you do not understand
- Consent is your right to information — do not feel pressured to sign quickly
- For surgical consents, ask the surgeon to explain the procedure in plain language

**Insurance / TPA Desk**
- Locate the TPA (Third Party Administrator) desk within the first 2 hours of admission
- Submit pre-authorization documents for cashless treatment before surgery, not after
- If TPA rejects cashless, ask for written rejection reason — this is required for reimbursement claim later
- Keep all original bills and receipts

---

## 5. Managing the First 24 Hours

**ICU Policy**
- Most ICUs in India allow 2 visiting slots per day (typically 10–11 AM and 5–6 PM, 15–30 minutes each)
- Only 1–2 visitors at a time; children under 12 are usually not permitted
- Ask the nursing station for the exact schedule on the first day

**Staying Connected**
- Note the nursing station phone number for the ward or ICU where patient is admitted
- The duty nurse is your primary point of contact for clinical updates between doctor rounds
- Doctor rounds typically happen in the morning (7–9 AM in most hospitals); be available at the ward at that time

**Practical Logistics**
- Locate the canteen, pharmacy, and restroom nearest to the ward on day one
- Identify the nearest ATM and a 24-hour chemist outside the hospital for medications not stocked in-house

---

## 6. What NOT to Do

- **Do not share medical details, photos, or condition updates on social media** — patient privacy; also causes unnecessary panic among extended family
- **Do not give any medication to the patient without doctor or nurse instruction** — including "home remedies" or supplements
- **Do not accept a verbal discharge without a written discharge summary** — insist on the printed discharge summary before leaving the hospital
- **Do not sign blank or partially completed forms** — fill completely or leave the field blank with a mark
- **Do not make treatment decisions based on information from other patients' families** — every case is different

---

## 7. Caregiver Wellbeing

Caregiving during a hospitalization is physically and emotionally exhausting. It is normal to feel overwhelmed, frightened, or even resentful. You cannot provide good care if you are running on empty.

- **Sleep in shifts** if multiple family members are present — someone should always be rested
- **Eat regular meals** — skipping meals impairs your judgment and stamina
- **Ask for help** — it is not weakness to involve other family members or friends
- **Hospital social workers** are available (in most tertiary hospitals) to help with emotional support, financial counseling, and family communication; ask the ward nurse to connect you with one
- If you feel unable to cope, ask hospital staff to connect you to the patient support / counseling team

---

## Guardrails

- This guide assists caregivers in navigating hospital systems; it does not replace clinical advice from treating doctors
- Any medical decision — medication change, procedure consent, discharge — must be made in consultation with the treating team
- Do not use this guide to second-guess clinical decisions; use it to ask better questions
